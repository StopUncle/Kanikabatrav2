## Brief

Kai has built a four-year-old SaaS at fourteen million ARR, sixty percent growth. Marc represents a strategic acquirer that needs Kai's category for a board narrative. Marc's opening number is forty million. The honest number is somewhere north of eighty. Kai must extract the additional value without giving Marc a reason to walk.

## Tactical anchors

This scenario teaches three moves:

1. **Silence as a negotiating tool.** A pause after a lowball is a counter-offer in itself. It costs nothing and forces the other side to fill the space.
2. **Counter-anchoring math.** The opener defines the range. A founder who replies to forty with forty-five has signed up for a range from forty to forty-five. A founder who replies to forty with eighty has reset the math.
3. **Urgency as a tell.** "We need to close by Friday" is almost always coercion. Real strategic deals run on multi-month timelines; the urgency framing is theatre designed to compress your think-time.

The optimal path uses silence plus a written counter-anchor at the number Kai actually believes. Failure paths involve emotional disclosure (anchoring on cost-of-living or the equity raised), premature acceptance (the LOI trap), and the theatrical walk-out (ego preserved, leverage lost).

## Voice notes

Tone: tactical, restrained, no melodrama. Marc speaks in the smooth register of a closer who has rehearsed every objection. Kai's inner voice is precise and short. No em-dashes.

Marc's tells: a small "fair" before he restates Kai's position back in his own framing (compression), a tendency to flatter the technology while compressing the price (split the founder's identity from the founder's company), a watch-glance when he wants Kai to feel time pressure.

Kai's optimal posture: weight forward, hands still, the silence held longer than feels comfortable. The counter-anchor is two lines, delivered as if it were obvious.
