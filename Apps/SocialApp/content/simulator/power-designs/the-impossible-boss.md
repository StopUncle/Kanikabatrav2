# The Impossible Boss

## Meta Information
- **Tier**: Free
- **Difficulty**: Intermediate
- **Estimated Scenes**: 28-35

## Logline

Your manager makes every day harder than it needs to be. Whether they're in your inbox every five minutes, taking credit for your wins, absent when you need them, or actively hostile—you're stuck. Quitting isn't an option yet. So you learn to manage up, survive, and maybe even thrive despite them.

---

## Core Theme

**You can't fire your boss, but you can manage them.** This scenario teaches:
- The four types of impossible bosses and how to handle each
- Managing up as a core professional skill
- When to adapt, when to document, when to escalate
- Protecting your career while trapped under bad management
- The long game: outlasting them

---

## Psychology Being Taught

### The Fundamental Reality
Most people don't quit jobs—they quit managers. But leaving isn't always an option. The skill of surviving and thriving under difficult management is one of the most valuable (and least taught) professional capabilities.

### The Four Impossible Boss Types

**1. The Micromanager**
- Checks on every detail, requests constant updates
- Doesn't trust you to do anything independently
- Cause: Anxiety, control issues, past betrayal
- Strategy: Over-communicate to build trust, manage their anxiety proactively

**2. The Credit Thief**
- Takes credit for your work, positions your ideas as theirs
- Blames you for failures, claims wins
- Cause: Insecurity, ambition at your expense
- Strategy: Document everything, build relationships above them

**3. The Absent Manager**
- Never available, no guidance, no support
- Leaves you to figure everything out alone
- Cause: Overwhelmed, checked out, or politically weak
- Strategy: Create your own structure, find other mentors

**4. The Hostile Manager**
- Publicly criticizes, plays favorites, or actively undermines
- May be threatened by you or just toxic
- Cause: Insecurity, personality disorder, or broken culture
- Strategy: Document, protect yourself, plan exit, consider escalation

### Professional vs. Dark Arts

| Situation | Professional Approach | Dark Arts Approach |
|-----------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Micromanager | Over-communicate, build trust | Make them dependent on your reporting, become their filter |
| Credit Thief | Document and share work widely | Build relationships above them, make your contributions visible |
| Absent Manager | Create your own structure, ask for what you need | Use their absence to build power, become the de facto leader |
| Hostile Manager | Document, involve HR if needed | Build alliances against them, make their behavior visible |

## Characters

### The Boss - Four Versions
The scenario adapts based on which boss type you're dealing with.

#### Version A: Jordan - The Micromanager
**Voice Example**:
> "Did you get my email from five minutes ago? I need that status update.
> Also, can you walk me through your process? Step by step."

**Tell**: Anxiety-driven. Not malicious, just terrified of being surprised.

---

#### Version B: Jordan - The Credit Thief
**Voice Example**:
> "Great work on the presentation. I'll present it to the board—
> easier for me to answer questions. I'll make sure to mention
> the team contributed."

**Tell**: Insecure. Building their career on your work.

---

#### Version C: Jordan - The Absent Manager
**Voice Example**:
> "Can't talk now. Let's reschedule. Actually, just handle it—
> I trust your judgment. Catch me up later."

**Tell**: Overwhelmed or checked out. Either way, you're on your own.

---

#### Version D: Jordan - The Hostile Manager
**Voice Example**:
> "I'm not sure you understood the assignment.
> Again. We talked about this. I'm starting to wonder
> if this role is the right fit for you."

**Tell**: Threatened, toxic, or just cruel. This isn't about your work.

---

### Sam - HR Partner
**Role**: May help, may protect the company, may do nothing.

**Voice Example** (Actually helpful):
> "I appreciate you bringing this to me. Let's talk about options."

**Voice Example** (Company protector):
> "I hear your concerns. Have you tried talking directly to Jordan?"

---

### Casey - The Skip Level (Jordan's Boss)
**Role**: Potential ally if relationship can be built carefully.

**Voice Example**:
> "How's it going under Jordan? I like to check in with the team."

---

### Morgan - Colleague/Ally
**Role**: Someone who sees what you see. Reality check. Support.

**Voice Example**:
> "It's not just you. Everyone on the team feels this way.
> You're not crazy."

**Location**: office


#### Scene 3M: The Strategy
**Location**: office

```
INNER VOICE:
Micromanagers need one thing: the feeling of control.

If you wait for them to ask, you trigger their anxiety.
If you proactively update, you calm it.

The paradox: more communication reduces their need to check on you.

NARRATION:
Strategy options:
```

**Choice 2M**:
1. **Proactive over-communication**: Send status before they ask, daily summaries, anticipate needs (+15 BOSS_RELATIONSHIP, +10 SURVIVAL_SCORE)
2. **Push back**: "I'll update you when there's something to report" (-15 BOSS_RELATIONSHIP, conflict)
3. **Match their energy**: Reply to every email immediately, feed the cycle (exhausting, neutral)
4. **Document the pattern**: Track every request, build case for skip-level (+15 DOCUMENTATION)

---

```
INNER VOICE:

Your work. Their credit. Their career.
```

---

#### Scene 3C: The Strategy
**Location**: office

```
INNER VOICE:
Credit thieves survive by being between you and visibility.

The solution: build direct relationships above them.
Make your work visible before it passes through their hands.

NARRATION:
Strategy options:
```

**Choice 2C**:
1. **Build skip-level relationships**: Find reasons to interact with Casey directly (+20 EXTERNAL_REPUTATION, +10 SURVIVAL_SCORE)
2. **Document and share widely**: CC leadership on key deliverables before Jordan presents (+15 DOCUMENTATION, +10 EXTERNAL_REPUTATION)
3. **Confront Jordan**: "I'd like to present my own work going forward" (+5 directly, may backfire)
4. **Accept and outlast**: They can't steal credit forever, focus on exit plan (+15 EXIT_READINESS)

#### Scene 3A: The Strategy
**Location**: office

```
INNER VOICE:
Absent managers leave a power vacuum.

You can flounder in it. Or fill it.

The aggressive read: their absence is your opportunity.
The practical read: build your own structure or drown.

NARRATION:
Strategy options:
```

**Choice 2A**:
1. **Create your own structure**: Set your own priorities, find other mentors, become self-managing (+20 SURVIVAL_SCORE, +10 EXTERNAL_REPUTATION)
2. **Fill the vacuum**: Start making decisions in their absence, become de facto leader (risky, +20 EXTERNAL_REPUTATION if works)
3. **Escalate to Casey**: "I need guidance and I'm not getting it from Jordan" (+10 DOCUMENTATION, risky)
4. **Force communication**: Keep booking meetings, escalate urgency until they respond (+10 BOSS_RELATIONSHIP attempt)

### PART 2: ADAPTATION (Examples for Micromanager Path)

---

#### Scene 5M: The Adjustment
**Location**: meeting-room

```
[One-on-one with Jordan]

JORDAN:
"I appreciate the updates. It helps me feel connected
to what's happening."

[pause]

JORDAN:
"I know I can be... detail-oriented."

INNER VOICE:
That's one word for it.

JORDAN:
"My last team had some issues. Surprised me with problems.
I'm trying to make sure that doesn't happen here."

INNER VOICE:
Their anxiety makes sense now. It's not about you.
It's about someone before you.

Understanding isn't forgiving. But it helps you manage.
```

**Choice 4M**:
1. **Acknowledge their experience**: "That sounds frustrating. I'm committed to keeping you informed." (+20 BOSS_RELATIONSHIP)
2. **Set a boundary gently**: "I hear you. Can we find a rhythm that works for both of us?" (+10 BOSS_RELATIONSHIP, +10 SURVIVAL_SCORE)
3. **Use the opening**: "What would help you feel most confident in the team?" (+15 BOSS_RELATIONSHIP)
4. **Stay tactical**: Note the intel, don't engage emotionally (neutral, protected)

## Endings

### EXCELLENT OUTCOMES

**1. Boss Changed**
**Trigger**: High BOSS_RELATIONSHIP, adapted strategy worked, boss modified behavior

```
NARRATION:
Jordan changed. Not completely, but enough.

The micromanaging eased when trust built.
The credit stealing stopped when you built visibility.
The absence reduced when you proved you needed them.

INNER VOICE:
Not every impossible boss is permanent.
Some just need to be managed.

LESSON:
Managing up is a skill. And like any skill,
it gets better with practice.
```

---

**2. Outlasted Them**
**Trigger**: High SURVIVAL_SCORE, boss left/fired/transferred

```
NARRATION:
Jordan is gone. Transferred. Or "transitioned."

The skip-level with Casey paid off.
The documentation proved a pattern.
The alliances made the decision easier.

INNER VOICE:
You didn't quit. They did.
Or they were helped to quit.

LESSON:
Sometimes the best strategy is survival.
Toxic managers don't last forever.
You just need to last longer.
```

---

**3. Clean Exit**
**Trigger**: High EXIT_READINESS, found better opportunity, left well

```
NARRATION:
The new job offer came through.
50% raise. Better title. Better manager.

You gave professional notice.
No drama. No burning bridges.

INNER VOICE:
You couldn't fix Jordan. So you fixed your situation.
That's not failure. That's strategy.

LESSON:
Not every battle is worth fighting.
Sometimes the win is a clean exit.
```

---

### GOOD OUTCOMES

**4. Survived and Learned**
**Trigger**: Moderate SURVIVAL_SCORE, grew from experience

```
NARRATION:
Jordan is still Jordan.
But you're different now.

You learned to manage up.
You learned to document.
You learned to build relationships above and around.

INNER VOICE:
They didn't break you.
And you picked up skills you'll use forever.

LESSON:
Bad bosses are expensive tuition.
Make sure you learn something for the price.
```

---

**5. Protected but Stagnant**
**Trigger**: High DOCUMENTATION, survived but not thriving

```
NARRATION:
You're safe. Not happy. Not growing. But safe.

The documentation protected you.
The skip-level gave you visibility.
But Jordan still blocks your path.

INNER VOICE:
Survival mode has limits.
How long can you stay here?

LESSON:
Protection is necessary. But it's not success.
At some point, you need to move.
```

---

### BAD OUTCOMES

**6. The Burnout**
**Trigger**: Low SURVIVAL_SCORE, didn't adapt, constant conflict

```
NARRATION:
Every day is a battle you're losing.

The micromanaging hasn't stopped.
The credit keeps being stolen.
The hostility keeps escalating.

And you're exhausted.

INNER VOICE:
You fought the current. It won.

LESSON:
Not every boss can be managed.
Sometimes the only move is to leave.
```

---

**7. The Trap**
**Trigger**: Didn't build EXIT_READINESS, stuck with no options

```
NARRATION:
Jordan is still here. So are you.

But you never updated your resume.
Never took recruiter calls.
Never built the network.

Now leaving is harder than staying.
And staying is destroying you.

INNER VOICE:
You waited too long to prepare.
The trap closed slowly.

LESSON:
Always have an exit. Even if you never use it.
The option to leave is power.
```

---

**8. The Retaliation**
**Trigger**: Escalated poorly, HR backfired, boss discovered complaint

```
NARRATION:
You went to HR. Jordan found out.

Now every interaction is ice.
Every assignment is designed to fail you.
Every review is carefully weaponized.

INNER VOICE:
Escalation without protection is suicide.
You had the right complaint. Wrong execution.

LESSON:
HR protects the company, not you.
Never escalate without documentation, witnesses, and an exit plan.
```
